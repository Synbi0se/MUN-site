The viaduc

ville-chaumont.fr

This is a remarkable viaduct, 600 meters long with 50 arches reaching 52 meters in height. Designed by architect Eugène Decomble, it was built in just 15 months between 1855 and 1856 to allow the railway to access the upper part of the city. The construction site employed 2,500 workers and 300 horses. Partially destroyed in 1944, it was quickly rebuilt. The viaduct is a symbol of the city, having been featured in several films. In 2012, it was given spectacular lighting.

The donjon

ville-chaumont.fr

The donjon, the only remaining part of the castle of the Lords of Chaumont, stands on a spur overlooking the Suize valley. This square tower, 19 meters high, was built in the 12th century and has retained its medieval appearance with thick walls. Initially defensive, the donjon lost a floor and served as a prison until 1886, leaving engraved inscriptions on its walls. It is accessible on request through the Médiévalys association. Nearby, there is a medieval-inspired garden and a path along the old ramparts.

The basilica

THIRION Louis

The Basilica of Saint-Jean(Saint John), located in the heart of the old medieval city, was initially built in the early 13th century, with part of the facade and nave still preserved. Over the centuries, additions were made, including a portal in the 14th century and side chapels between the 15th and early 16th centuries. The transept and choir, rebuilt between 1517 and 1543, combine flamboyant Gothic elements and early Renaissance. The basilica's furniture is remarkable, including a Tomb (late 15th-early 16th century), works by Jean-Baptiste Bouchardon, a Tree of Jesse from the 1530s, 16th-century murals, and paintings from the 17th, 18th, and 19th centuries. The basilica also houses a 1872 Cavaillé-Coll romantic organ, with a case dating from the second half of the 18th century.

The Jesuit chapel

ville-chaumont.fr

The Jesuit chapel, built between 1629 and 1640 thanks to donations from local families, is a typical example of Jesuit architecture. Its interior is richly decorated, with a monumental altarpiece by Claude Collignon and a high relief added in the 19th century by Jean-Baptiste Bouchardon. The facade was redone in 1817 by architect Mangot. Next to the chapel is the Bouchardon fountain, in homage to the sculptor Edme Bouchardon. After the Revolution, the chapel served as a place of worship for the first high school in Chaumont and now hosts contemporary art exhibitions, being open to visitors in the summer.

The turrets

ville-chaumont.fr

The turrets, emblematic of Chaumont, intrigue passersby who explore the old town. Once very numerous, about thirty remain today, not counting those hidden in courtyards. These structures, studied in architecture schools, vary in shape (square or circular) and style (with or without roofs, simple or decorated facades). Some even house statuettes in niches. Built to optimize public space, the turrets served as entrance vestibules and stairways to the upper floors.

The town hall

THIRION Louis

The town hall of Chaumont, built between 1787 and 1790, replaced the old Barle tower, which had become too small for the municipal administration of a town of 6,200 inhabitants. Designed by architect François-Nicolas Lancret, the building reflects the spirit of the time with its straight lines, regular curves, and sober symmetry. A model, displayed at the Museum of Art and History, shows the initial plans, including a small theater, abandoned during the Revolution. Listed in the supplementary inventory of Historic Monuments, the Town Hall has been illuminated since 2012 to highlight the elegance of its facade.

The market halls

ville-chaumont.fr

The Chaumont market halls, built between 1883 and 1886 by the Chaumont architect Dupuy, are a typical example of late 19th-century architecture, in the Baltard style. Using cast iron, this innovative material made it possible to create vast spaces with large glass openings. The Market Halls were erected on the site of an old grain market, itself built in 1799 on the site of the Saint-Michel church, demolished the same year. This church, dating from the 14th century and expanded in the 17th century, had served as a prison and military store during the Revolution. The Market Halls, renovated in 2004, now host various events, including a food market every Saturday morning.

The Crèche museum

.

The Crèche museum, dedicated to the celebration of Christmas, houses the finest collection of 18th-century Baroque Neapolitan nativity scenes in France. Located in the heart of the historic city, this museum offers a festive experience for all ages with a collection of nativity scenes, figurines, and other Christmas decorations from different parts of the world. The museum also hosts seasonal events and workshops for children.

The silos

.

The Silos (house of books and posters) is a multimedia library. The name of it reminds the origin of the building which was a cooperative of farmers, characteristic of the 30's architecture. The project of rehabilitation let the seed hopper to stay, which cross all over the floors. The silos keeps a massive collection : 5 000 posters from the end of the XIXth century from the legacy Dutailly, more than 15 000 contemporary posters, 450 manuscrpits and incunabulars.

The Signe

.

Le Signe, National Center of Graphic design, is dedicated to the promotion and the exploration of graphic design in every shape and form. Opened in 2016, he is based on the city's tradition, notably thanks to the international festival of posters and graphic design, launched in 1990. Le Signe povide exhibitions, workshops and conferences to celebrate graphic design as visual art and way of communication. It is also home to an international poster collection, causing Chaumont to be an important cultural pole of graphic design.

The hospital

.

In 1716, Jean-Bastiste Bouchardon designs a poject for an hospital and a building out of Chaumont to isolate the patient so they can profiter de l'air de la campagne. Plus tard, l'architecte Claude Forgeot réduit et reprend les plans, et la reconstruction est terminée en 1765. La façade sobre est marquée par une entrée monumentale de la chapelle, avec des bâtiments symétriques de chaque côté. À l'intérieur, la chapelle présente une nef à deux travées menant à un chœur circulaire surmonté d'un dôme. Un retable en bois doré, réalisé par Bouchardon vers 1730, orne le chœur.

The Boulingrin garden

.

The Boulingrin garden, arrange in the middle of the XIXe century after the dismantling of the fortification, is decorated by a gigantic foutain acquired in 1865 in the foundry of Tusey. The fountain with its nymph and her love add a touch of elegance to the garden, in which the clear water create an harmonious scenery merging foundery and flowering. The garden also has a replica of the statue "l'Amour taillant ses flèches", realised by the sculptor Edme Bouchardon from Chaumont, as well as a bandstand.

The Arse tower

THIRION Louis

The Arse tower, vestige of the gate of "la Voie-de-l'Eau" and part of the second fortification belt of the XVIe siècle, was setting the entrance of Chaumont. Once arsenal of the town, it became a backery and acquired again by the city in 1985. Refurbished, it gets back its initial appearance, with a terrific wooden framework. The tower is build up against a 200 meters high fortification wich link to the bastion of the castle.

The prefecture

.

The building of the prefecture of Chaumont is a remarkable exemple of XIXth century architecture. It is known for its neoclassic style, with massive architectural element such as columns, pediments and elaborated decorative elements. The building is made to reflect the dignity and the authority of governemental instructions. Its majestic frontage and its harmonious proportions is making it as a figure of the importance of the prefecture as a administrative center of the departement of Haute-Marne.

The Juvet street

adventuringthegreatwidesomewhere.com

Renovated in the 90's, the Juvet street reflect the ambiance of the ancient faubourgs of Chaumont with its small houses, staircase turrets, colored frontage and small gardens. It is named on the honnor of "Hugues-Alexis Juvet", doctor and intendant of the Waters, who helped during the plague wave of 1741. In acknowledgement, the motto "Nihil Ni Juvet" was engraved on the side of his house. Louise Michel, figure of the "Commune de Paris", also stayed for a day in this street around 1850-1851 as a boarder of Ms Beths, preparing the primary teaching certificate.
